Meet the Educators

Each teacher brought different values, different subjects, and different classroom structures to the same question: what do you do when your students have access to a tool that can do the work you assigned them?

About This Study

In 2024, Anthony E. Wolf — a teacher himself — conducted a qualitative study at the University of San Francisco examining how seven Bay Area high school educators responded to the arrival of large language models (LLMs) in their classrooms.

Wolf set out to understand three core questions: How does a teacher’s pedagogy shape their view of LLMs? What measures have teachers taken in response? And how does student identity factor into that response?

To evaluate these questions, Wolf used Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) as his primary theoretical lens. The ZPD describes the space between what a learner can accomplish independently and what they can achieve with guidance or the support of tools. Wolf’s central argument is that the critical question is not whether AI exists in the classroom — but whether it expands a student’s capacity for thinking or replaces it entirely. A tool that scaffolds the learning process will fit the ZPD model. A tool that substitutes for it will not.

Wolf (2024) observed that teachers clustered into three broad camps based on how they perceived LLMs fitting — or not fitting — into their existing pedagogy. These aren't rigid categories, but useful orientations: those who felt AI actively disrupted what they were building, those who didn't engage with the question directly, and those who moved toward integration. Jim sees the shift happening but doubles down on enforcement, despite predicting it won't hold.

No, and It Actively Uproots the Process

Jim practiced a scaffolded math model, providing support on step-by-step processes and teaching fundamental skills that build on each other. He believes this will enable students to gain stronger intellectual authority and extend their own thinking. However, he felt LLMs threatened this model directly. He drew contrast to Wolfram Alpha, a program first introduced in 2009 on which his school had already developed an academic integrity policy. Wolfram Alpha provides complete step-by-step solutions to math problems, drawing on virtually the full breadth of mathematical knowledge — however, if students try to follow this method to complete their assignments, they usually do not understand the work they have copied from the tool, and it may implement mathematical techniques the student has not yet learned or understood. LLMs presented the same problem at higher fidelity and with less detectability. Teaching virtually, Jim noted that “being virtual exacerbates the distrust that can form by being distant from students and unable to more directly engage them from their computers.” He banned AI, sent students to study hall to explain their work or face a zero and a dean’s meeting. But in the same breath as defending his policy, he predicted administrators would get overwhelmed as more students figured out the tool was easier than the work.
Reflection

Jim drew contrast between Wolfram Alpha and LLMs during his interview with Wolf. He described how Wolfram Alpha “can give step-by-step processes to solving math problems using all the knowledge of mathematics that is available, and rarely makes mistakes except at the highest levels of math.”

Can you think of any direct ways features from Wolfram Alpha that might translate to AI? What might not translate at all? He also noted that students who relied on Wolfram Alpha “usually do not understand the work they have copied from the tool.” How do you think Jim could tell whether a student had only used it to copy an answer rather than learn the concept? Might anything similar apply to AI?

Jim values putting students in positions to achieve early and often. He believes the key to accomplishing that is to provide heavy support on detailed step-by-step components and reinforcing fundamental concepts.

Can you think of a time when someone gave you specific instructions, but you needed it explained a different way — perhaps through an analogy, a visual aid, a connection to your own life, or simplified terms you could understand? What about with a larger concept?

Given the gap between Jim and his students’ AI experience, what impact do you think developing AI fluency might have on his classroom culture and his ability to communicate effectively with his students?

Denise feels AI undermines the core skill she’s working to develop — though she has used it effectively herself.

No, and It Actively Uproots the Process

Denise structured her entire model around outlining as the foundation for developing a student’s own analytical voice, but increasingly she is discovering that her “pedagogical techniques require student effort in a way that is incompatible with the assistance of LLMs.” She expressed deep concern that “students could use LLMs to eliminate their voice in assignments.” Her concern was philosophically grounded — LLMs flatten everything into a generic authoritative register by default, when the student’s own thinking and voice aren’t present in the input. When students are caught, she assigns a zero and flags it in the weekly letter she sends to families. Crucially, Denise found a practical use for an LLM when personally faced with a challenge in her own classroom. In a time crunch and unable to watch a video she had assigned to a student, she used the video transcript alongside ChatGPT to develop comprehension questions for the assignment. She understood the tool’s utility as a practitioner, but hasn’t extended that framework to her students, making her valid concern a self-fulfilling prophecy.
Reflection

Denise perceived that students were more confident in the LLM’s answer than in their own thinking — and would lie to her when she asked if ChatGPT had written their response.

Why do you think students might feel more confident in the LLM’s answer than their own?

If a student didn’t truly understand the concept, how could they explain the answer under scrutiny without going back to the LLM?

How could Denise be sure whether a student was lying — or actually explaining their own thinking?

Jane treats AI as just the latest thing to channel thoughtfully, not a crisis to manage.

Yes, and We Need to Embrace That Future

A project-based learning teacher who also worked as a professional journalist, Jane’s class produced the school’s actual magazine. Students had real editorial roles, real deadlines, real consequences for miscommunication. When a student discovered LLMs and wanted to present about them to the class, Jane let it happen, guided the discussion, and came out with clearer norms the students themselves had shaped. One detection method Jane practiced was the utilization of the track changes feature on Google Docs, which allowed her to see whether assignments were written gradually over time or appeared all at once — not as a gotcha, but as a conversation starter. When she caught a student using ChatGPT improperly due to time pressure, she asked them to reflect on what they could have done differently and let them redo the assignment with points off. She also graded senior editorial students on how well they managed their underclassmen — resolving conflict, communicating expectations, holding standards. She was on her district's AI task force, and felt strongly about the lack of clear school-level policy, actively working to help shape one.
Reflection

Wolf notes a similarity in both Jane and Calvin’s pedagogy, observing that “both Jane and Calvin participate in a project-based learning that is more flexible in process.” What differences do you notice in how they interact with their students about AI? Which group of students do you think will leave their class better equipped for a world where AI is present in their professional landscape?

In the same passage where he draws comparisons between Jane and Calvin’s pedagogy, Wolf also mentions a shared quality in their classroom environments that lends itself naturally to navigating AI. What do you think that quality might be? What would it look like in your own context?

What are some potential blind spots or downsides to Jane’s approach that she may be missing? How might you address them if you were Jane?

Lily accepts AI is in her students' hands and builds a practical strategy around that reality.

Yes, and We Need to Embrace That Future

A caring teacher navigating a restrictive Honors curriculum, Lily had to operate under tension as AI arrived in her classroom. In marine biology, students raised real fish. "They're not even on the standards — it's how to raise a fish and not kill it." She permitted AI to simplify concepts and generate practice problems, but not to replace student voice in writing. When students crossed that line, she didn't hesitate to call it out directly: "Are you gonna write your own thing?…Come on, guys, I know — if it's AI, it's kind of obvious." One of her detection methods: white text hidden in assignments that instructed any LLM to use the word "coconuts." Students who copy-pasted without reading triggered it immediately. She acknowledged this wasn't a foolproof system — but a filter for the most egregious cases, not a catch-all.
Reflection

Can you think of reasons why Lily feels that “if it’s AI, it’s kind of obvious,” when other teachers have expressed exasperation about the same thing?

Denise says AI “produces master’s level stuff” that students don’t understand. Lily’s Honors marine biology curriculum lends itself to plenty of master’s-level content that is featured in the data sets LLMs draw from. If Lily applied Denise’s approach, what are some things that might change in her classroom?

Consider Lily’s “Are you gonna write your own thing? Come on — I know it’s AI” comment to a student. Compare this to Jim’s approach, where he threatens punishment whenever he suspects AI use. As the school year goes on, how do you think these situations will affect their relationships with students and their ability to reach them? How might that evolving dynamic affect student learning?

Michael believes his teachers have more of the answer than they think — his job is just to surface it.

Yes, and We Need to Embrace That Future

Michael no longer taught students directly — he coached teachers. His philosophy was equity grading: students should retest, submit late, and show mastery through multiple means. On AI, his take was consistent with how he approached everything — students already have the tools, the question is how you train them to use them responsibly. As a veteran of two decades in the classroom, he drew parallels to Google and Wikipedia — tools he'd watched arrive and eventually get absorbed. He described observing a teacher who gave a student zero but gave "the AI" 50 points on the same rubric — complete with sarcastic comments on the AI's work — to illustrate that the output was valuable but the student hadn't done it. His approach to teachers mirrored his approach to students: point out what's already working rather than impose new frameworks.
Reflection

Michael believes most teachers are already doing more right than they realize — his job is to surface it and connect it to the shifting landscape around them.

Given his philosophy, what practical advice do you think Michael might give to Denise? To Jim?

If you personally were in Michael’s seat with your own current philosophy, what advice would you give to Denise and Jim?

What are some techniques you think Michael may have used 20 years ago when Google and Wikipedia arrived in his classroom? Do you think any of those elements could potentially translate to the current landscape?

Calvin feels he lacks enough personal experience with AI to make a defensible policy decision.

I Don't Have Time for This

Calvin admitted openly that he couldn't tell AI-generated work from student work — and because he couldn't, he felt he had no legitimate basis for a punitive policy. He asked students to cite AI use but had never seen anyone do it. His mock trial pedagogy — draft, receive feedback, redraft, argue solo in front of the class — is actually a naturally conducive structure for AI-adapted assessment. But he hadn't connected those dots.
Reflection

Calvin admitted that there is a widening gap between his students’ methods and his own, mentioning that he was “reluctant to engage with the use of LLMs proactively.”

Do you think Calvin is satisfied with keeping things status quo for the foreseeable future? What advice would you give to him?

Long-term, what effects might come from Calvin not proactively engaging with the tool as AI evolves and new innovations emerge?

“I give them a lot of examples on how to do [a mock trial]. They draft something, they receive my feedback, they redraft it... Then gradually I pull back until hopefully they can feel confident doing it on their own in front of me for a final summative assessment.”

Do you think if Calvin developed AI fluency he might improve his ability to effectively manage the gap between both his and his students’ current methods? How?

Amy's students have different developmental priorities entirely — the AI question simply doesn't intersect her work.

I Don't Have Time for This

Amy worked one-on-one with students with IEPs (Individualized Education Plans), primarily managing behavioral needs related to autism diagnoses. Her primary goal was keeping students safe and regulated enough to participate in learning at all. The AI question simply never arrived in her work — not because she opposed it, but because the problems she was solving and the problems AI addresses don’t overlap. Wolf’s theoretical point is elegant: the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) AI creates runs perpendicular to her students’ needs. Their developmental zone is emotional regulation and behavioral participation. AI doesn’t scaffold that, and is not a magic wand. Context determines everything.

Spotlight

Michael’s Philosophy — Meet Them Where They Are

Michael’s approach as a teacher-coach is worth examining separately. It’s the one most directly applicable to anyone trying to help others navigate AI in a wide variety of professional settings with different audiences.

The Coach Model

Don’t Impose a New Framework — Surface the One They Already Have

With 20 years of experience, Michael had developed a specific way of working with teachers that mirrors what the best AI adoption looks like in any organization. He wasn’t trying to revolutionize anyone’s practice. He was helping them see what they already did well and point it in a slightly better direction.

Start with existing practice

He observed what teachers already did and named what was working. No new systems required before you begin. “A lot of the practices they’re already doing.”

What is 1 thing that you are already doing in your regular workflow that bogs you down?

Make direct connections

“I just have to point out what they’re doing to each other.” He didn’t lecture. He reflected back what was already visible, and connected it to the shifting landscape around them.

Try to make your own connection. Close your eyes and visualize each step of that workflow. Can you imagine any scenario where incorporating AI somewhere — in any capacity, at any point — might improve the process?

Sit with Michael’s question

If a student uses AI and the output earns full marks — but the teacher can’t see the input — what is actually being assessed? Is the grade measuring the student’s thinking, or the tool’s output?

Ask yourself: what is my role in this workflow? Which tasks require me and my unique skills?

Assessment is designed to measure a student’s thinking — not just the accuracy of an answer. Michael’s observation surfaces a problem AI makes visible but didn’t create. A student might have engineered a precise, thoughtful prompt that reflects genuine understanding. Or they might have typed three words and accepted whatever came back. The output may look identical. The learning isn’t. The teacher who graded the AI’s work “50 out of 50” didn’t have access to the student’s input — and that gap is the real dilemma. If the student’s input was essentially nothing, then grading the output generously doesn’t assess the student at all. It assesses the tool. Which raises the question: in an environment that sees varying levels of AI use, what defines quality work? How can you know?

The Hard Question

What if you understand all of this — and you still don't want to use it?

This isn't a hypothetical. Environmental concerns are real and documented. Societal concerns are real and documented. The feeling of not wanting to participate in something you find problematic — even if you benefit from it — is legitimate and worth taking seriously.

But here's the tension the case studies surface: is it increasingly necessary for professionals themselves to develop AI fluency in order to continue doing their jobs effectively as things change around them? And if the answer is yes — what do you do when your personal values say no?

Calvin's Dilemma: A Scenario

Imagine Calvin not just uncertain — but actively opposed to AI on principle. He still teaches law. His students still prepare for a world where AI is standard in legal practice. He still has the preparation gap. His personal objection doesn't change what his students need or what his classroom requires. Further inaction in a rapidly changing environment will only widen the gap between Calvin's methods and his students'. The paralysis we observed gets harder, not easier, when the resistance is intentional rather than circumstantial. Consider: being fluent in a different language wouldn't change your nationality — but it would change your ability to reach people, and likely your overall perspective on communication. AI fluency works the same way.

The Minimum Effective Dose Question

Everyone has a minimum effective dose — and context shapes what that looks like. Calvin's situation and Amy's sit at opposite ends of this: AI simply doesn't intersect Amy's students' developmental needs, so her dose is effectively zero. Calvin's students are heading into a profession where AI is already standard practice — his dose looks very different. The case studies suggest Calvin's path forward doesn't require enthusiastic adoption. It requires enough personal experience to know what skilled use looks like in his domain. Building one legal argument using AI wouldn't make him an AI advocate. It would give him the evaluative capacity to recognize the difference between a student who prepared deeply and one who avoided thinking. That's a much lower bar than endorsement.

Purposeful vs. Shallow — A Frame for the Reluctant

The distinction between shallow and purposeful use isn't just for enthusiasts. For someone with genuine objections, it might be the most useful frame of all. It creates space to say: I'm not going to use this casually or habitually. But I will use it in specific contexts, where it genuinely changes what I can do. That's not hypocrisy. That's a considered position — and it's really no different from the cost analysis your brain inherently runs in every decision you make. Real costs, real benefits, deliberate choice.

The Question No One Asks Directly

At what point does not engaging with a tool that your field, your students, or your colleagues are all using become its own kind of professional choice with its own consequences? This isn't a question with a clean answer — context, role, and timing all shape what it actually means for any individual. But it's worth deeply examining your own relationship with the tool.
